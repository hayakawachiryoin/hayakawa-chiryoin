Japanese Healing Traditions

What is Shiatsu & Acupuncture?

Unlike Western massage, Japanese shiatsu and acupuncture treat the root cause of your pain — not just the symptoms. These healing arts have been refined over centuries and are recognized by the WHO as effective therapies.

Traditional Japanese

指圧マッサージ Shiatsu & Massage

Shiatsu (指圧, "finger pressure") is a Japanese therapeutic bodywork technique developed from traditional Chinese medicine. Using thumbs, fingers, and palms, the therapist applies rhythmic pressure along the body's energy meridians to release tension, restore balance, and promote natural healing.

  • Releases deep muscle tension and chronic stiffness
  • Improves blood circulation and lymphatic flow
  • Reduces stress, fatigue, and jet lag
  • Restores the body's natural energy balance (Qi)
  • No needles — suitable for needle-sensitive patients
Traditional Oriental Medicine

鍼治療 Acupuncture

Japanese acupuncture (鍼治療) uses ultra-thin, sterile needles to stimulate specific points on the body. Unlike Chinese-style acupuncture, the Japanese approach emphasizes precise, gentle palpation before each needle — Hayakawa's signature "3-second needle" technique reaches the root of pain with remarkable accuracy.

  • Targets the root cause, not just the symptom
  • Ultra-fine Japanese needles — minimal discomfort
  • Effective for back pain, sports injuries, chronic pain
  • Recognized by WHO as evidence-based therapy
  • Combined with shiatsu for maximum benefit

About the Director

Hayakawa Kazuhiro

早川 和浩 · Director, Hayakawa Therapy Clinic

Hayakawa
Kazuhiro

早川 和浩  ·  Founder & Director  ·  Opened September 2008

After earning his NATA (National Athletic Trainers' Association) certification in 1997, Hayakawa Kazuhiro made history by becoming the first Japanese assistant trainer in MLB history, joining the Detroit Tigers for the 1999–2000 seasons.

Returning to Japan, he served as Head Trainer for OSG Phoenix Basketball (2001–2003) and as a full-time instructor at Trident Sports Medicine School (2003–2007), training the next generation of Japan's athletic trainers.

In 2008, he was selected as trainer for the Japan Weightlifting Team at the Beijing Olympics. Since opening Hayakawa Therapy Clinic in 2008, he has continued to serve as active team trainer for top Japanese professional sports teams while treating private patients with the same world-class standard of care.
